
# Introduction to Work Logs ğŸ““

A work log is an important tool for documenting your work. A work log is in the spirit of a laboratory notebook (Kanare, 1985), which is used by scientists and engineers to document their work in great detail. Writing in a work log helps the researcher to stop and think about what they are working on. Too often researchers can be caught up in the moment and forget to document their work. As Kanare notes, "Writing good notes requires discipline and practice. It is a skill that does not come easily to most people." (Kanare, 1985, p. 13)

A work log provides a means for keeping track of and documenting the work accomplished on a project. Think of the work log as a diary of your efforts and a record of your decision-making. The work log is for the individual researcher, but it also provides a way to communicate with colleagues, collaborators, and (in this class) with me.

> **ğŸ’¡ Tacit expectation (made explicit):** The work log is not a productivity report and it is not a polished deliverable. It is a thinking space, a memory aid, and a communication tool.

## Types of Work Logs ğŸ“‚

The work log can be organized in many ways. In general, it is best to have a work log for each project or task. In this class, you will typically maintain at least two work logs:

- **Tasks work log** (for course setup, exercises, and logistics)
- **Project work log** (for your individual research project)

A strong recommendation is to use a digital file that allows for adding screenshots of issues, notes on work, notes from meetings, links to code or repositories, and short reflections on what worked and what did not.

> **ğŸ¯ Tacit expectation (made explicit):** If something does not work, that is not a failure. Documenting why it did not work is often more valuable than documenting a success.

## Ideas for Organization ğŸ—‚ï¸

- **Name** your work log with the project mnemonic followed by _WorkLog[Initials]
- **Put** the most recent entry at the top of the document
- **Use dates** as headings (e.g., ## 2026-01-25) so the document outline can be used for navigation
- **Create** multiple entries for that day if you do multiple distinct tasks
- **Keep** the work log simpleâ€”it does not need to be written in a formal style or read like a report

> **ğŸ¯ Tacit expectation (made explicit):** A good work log entry often answers three questions:
> - What did I try?
> - What happened (including errors or surprises)?
> - What do I plan to do next?


## What to Include in a Work Log Entry ğŸ“

A work log entry may include:

- A short summary of what you worked on
- Screenshots of error messages or results
- Snippets of code or links to scripts/notebooks
- Notes on AI tools used (including prompts and outcomes)
- Estimates of time spent
- Reflections such as "this took longer than expected" or "this part was confusing"

> **âœï¸ Tacit expectation (made explicit):** It is perfectly acceptable to write an entry that says you opened the files, got stuck, and did not make progress. Honesty and transparency matter more than speed.
